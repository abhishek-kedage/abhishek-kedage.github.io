01 Stability Control · CarSim
4WID-MPC Vehicle Stability Control
Electric vehicles with independently driven wheels can control each wheel's torque individually — giving direct authority over yaw moment without a mechanical differential. This project implements a two-layer hierarchical controller to exploit that freedom for yaw stability under aggressive maneuvers and low-adhesion surfaces.

The upper layer is a Mamdani fuzzy logic controller that takes yaw-rate error and sideslip-angle error as inputs and generates a corrective yaw moment command. The lower layer is a Model Predictive Controller that allocates this corrective moment across the four wheels by solving a constrained quadratic program at every timestep using a custom Hildreth dual method solver — no Optimization Toolbox required, making it suitable for code generation.

The full 7-DOF vehicle plant (longitudinal, lateral, yaw, and four wheel rotational dynamics) was co-simulated in CarSim, with a second-order motor model at each wheel to capture actuator bandwidth. Tested across three ISO maneuvers: tight double-lane-change (DLC) at 120 km/h, low-μ DLC at 80 km/h (μ = 0.3), and sinusoidal steering at 100 km/h.
Yaw rate 144 → 47 deg/s (DLC) 75% sideslip reduction Spin-out prevented at μ = 0.3

02 Powertrain · Energy Management
Hybrid / EV / ICE Powertrain Simulation
A forward-facing vehicle simulation framework built in Simulink that models, simulates, and compares three powertrain architectures for a passenger sedan under real-world EPA drive cycles.

The ICE model uses a BSFC lookup map with a 6-speed automatic transmission and a PID driver block for speed tracking. Gear-shift logic is optimized to keep the engine near its peak-efficiency island. The BEV model pairs a BorgWarner HVH250 motor with a 40.8 kWh Thevenin battery pack and PID-controlled thermal management. The series-parallel HEV combines a 1.5L engine with an HPEVS AC-23 motor for 90 kW combined output.

The HEV energy management strategy is implemented as a rule-based Stateflow controller with two driver-selectable modes — Economy and Sport — managing engine ON/OFF, motor torque split, clutch state, regenerative braking activation, and battery charging. The Economy mode optimizes fuel economy by keeping the engine in its BSFC sweet spot and maximizing EV-only operation at low power demands.

03 BMS · DOE + Stellantis
BMS — SOC & SOP Estimation
Developed as part of the U.S. DOE Battery Workforce Challenge in collaboration with Stellantis, this project covers BMS algorithm development and thermal controls design for a 110 kWh production vehicle battery pack.

SOC estimation uses an Extended Kalman Filter observer on a Thevenin RC equivalent circuit model. The state vector contains SOC and the RC polarization voltage. Temperature-dependent parameters — OCV curve, internal resistance R0, and RC time constant — are stored as 2D lookup tables and updated at each cycle. The EKF was validated against live cell testing data from the Clemson lab.

SOP estimation applies a voltage-constraint method: given the current SOC and temperature, the maximum discharge current is found by solving for the current that drives terminal voltage to V_min over the prediction horizon. A parallel computation gives maximum charge power. Both are computed in real-time from the same Thevenin model parameters.

The charging controller is a Stateflow state machine managing CC-CV charging protocol with temperature and voltage safety interlocks. Thermal design sized coolant channels for a peak heat load of 8.9 kW under aggressive driving.
110 kWh production pack EKF SOC observer 8.9 kW thermal design

04 Thermal · Formula SAE
Motor Thermal Management System
During testing of the HPEVS AC-23 motor (32 kW peak) for the Formula SAE electric race car, sustained load was causing the motor to exceed its 80°C rated temperature, reaching 85°C and triggering controller shutdowns. The original forced-air cooling was inadequate.

A thermal resistance network was first built analytically to size the cooling circuit — iterating on channel geometry to achieve ΔT ≤ 8°C coolant rise while minimizing pressure drop. The aluminum cooling jacket was then designed in CATIA V5 as a serpentine-channel wrap around the motor stator housing and validated through CFD in ANSYS Fluent before manufacturing, confirming 15 kPa pressure drop and a uniform temperature distribution with no hotspots.

A PID cooling controller on Arduino modulates pump speed based on real-time stator temperature from an NTC thermistor. A low-pass filter on the thermistor signal removes vibration-induced noise. Running the pump at minimum required speed rather than continuously reduced pump energy consumption by 40%.

The car — Alectrona 2.0 — completed all dynamic events at Formula Bharat and finished 2nd place nationally.

05 ADAS · Embedded Control
Sensor Fusion & Adaptive Cruise Control
This project implements a complete autonomous driving stack on an RC vehicle — dual ultrasonic sensor fusion, Kalman filter state estimation, and two independent PID controllers for throttle and steering — all running on Arduino at approximately 200 Hz.

Each HC-SR04 sensor was individually calibrated using linear regression across the full 20–1500 mm range, achieving residuals under 2 mm. A Kalman filter (measurement noise R = 8 μs variance) reduces noise on each channel, and the two filtered readings are fused using inverse-variance weighting. Dual-sensor fusion reduced convergence time from 600 ms to 250 ms compared to a single sensor.

The ACC PID controller (Kp=0.8, Ki=0.04, Kd=2.3) maintains a 30 cm following gap. The lane-keeping PID (Kp=2.0, Ki=0.01, Kd=1.8) holds lateral position, with a weighted-average filter on the steering output to prevent servo hunting from noisy lateral readings. The vehicle completed a 5-checkpoint test track in 12.6 seconds.

06 Electromagnetic Machines · Simulink
Electric Machine Modelling — Linear, ABC & DQ Frame
Three machine models built from first principles in Simulink, progressing from a linear electromagnetic actuator to a full three-phase induction machine in two different reference frames — abc phase and dq reference frame.

The linear machine model applies Newton's law and Faraday's law to describe the coupled electro-mechanical dynamics of a moving-coil actuator. The model outputs velocity, position, force, mechanical power, field energy, and coil current under step force and voltage inputs. Spring stiffness (K = 2667 N/m), damping (D = 4 N·s/m), and mass (0.055 kg) are parameterized for a realistic actuator.

The abc phase induction machine models stator and rotor voltages per phase using Faraday's law and Newton's rotational law. Six coupled voltage equations (3 stator + 3 rotor) drive flux linkage states, from which electromagnetic torque and rotor speed are computed. Supplied at 460 V / 60 Hz, 4-pole configuration.

The DQ reference frame model transforms the abc equations into the synchronously rotating dq frame, decoupling the coupled phase equations into a more computationally efficient state-space form. The Torque vs RPM curve obtained from simulation matches the expected induction machine characteristic — high starting torque with oscillatory transient, settling to a stable operating point.

07 Battery Pack · Formula SAE
Formula SAE — Accumulator Design
The accumulator is the most safety-critical subsystem of a Formula SAE electric vehicle. This project covers the complete ground-up design of the E06 accumulator for Formula Bharat 2022 — from lap energy simulation through cell chemistry selection, pack architecture, segment mechanical design, BMS integration, and a full Simulink modelling suite.

Energy requirements were determined using OpenLap simulation of the full vehicle model on the Formula Bharat track (1046 m), yielding 5.62 kWh for the endurance distance with a 10% margin → 6.2 kWh pack. After surveying 8 cells across 4 manufacturers, Melasta SLPBA390190 NMC pouch cells (21 Ah, 15C discharge) were selected. Pack configuration was iterated across 4 segment arrangements to satisfy FS rulebook limits (max 12 kg, 120 VDC, 6 MJ per segment), settling on 5 × 4s4p segments with 80 cells total.

All segment components — pressure plates, FR4 busbars, tab washers, side walls — were analyzed in ANSYS to confirm survival under 40g longitudinal/lateral and 20g vertical acceleration. The Simulink suite covers cell modelling (Thevenin RC), full 20s4p pack, CC-CV charging (cell and pack), and I²R heat dissipation per segment row for cooling fan selection.
72V · 6.2 kWh · 80 cells 40g FEA validated Orion BMS 2 · Melasta NMC
